I've been keeping a private log for about five years on where AI is and where it seems to be going. I read it back every few months and laugh. <mark>I've been wrong about timelines constantly. I've been right about direction more than I expected.</mark> This is me trying to write down the parts I feel most settled on, with room to be wrong in public.

I'm not a researcher. I don't work at a lab. I build Shopify stores and digital products, and I use these tools every day to do it. That vantage point is limited in some ways and clarifying in others. I see what actually works when you're trying to ship something, not what looks impressive in a demo.

## The current plateau is misleading

Every time a new model ships and feels only marginally better than the last one, people say progress is slowing. I've heard this three or four times now. Each time it was wrong.

<mark>What's slowing is the visible part. What isn't slowing is the interior work.</mark> Tooling, reliability, long-horizon tasks, agents that can run unsupervised for more than ten minutes without going sideways. Those improvements are less photogenic. They don't make good tweets. But they're the ones that determine whether you can actually hand something to a model and walk away.

Here's the framing I keep coming back to. A model that's 98% reliable at a task is effectively useless for anything you'd run unattended. A model that's 99.9% reliable at the same task changes your entire workflow. <mark>Those two numbers look identical from the outside and feel miles apart from the inside.</mark> That's where most of the real progress has been happening. Not on the benchmark that makes the headline.

I've been on the wrong side of this before. In 2022 I thought we were close to a ceiling on what language models could do with code. I was embarrassingly wrong. I try to hold my current views a little looser because of it.

## What actually got better in the last year

Not observations from someone reading papers. Things I noticed in my actual work.

<mark>Long context became usable, not just advertised.</mark> A year ago, stuffing a large codebase or a full client brief into a prompt would result in the model quietly ignoring the second half. Now it mostly doesn't. That changed what I can ask for in a single turn and it changed it a lot.

Tool use stopped being a gamble. Function calling used to fail often enough that you had to build error handling around every single call and still couldn't trust the output. Now it fails rarely enough that I've stopped treating it as the fragile part of the system. That's not a small shift. That's the difference between a prototype and something you can actually build on.

<mark>Pasting a screenshot and asking a question became normal.</mark> I know how that sounds. But a year ago I would describe an interface problem in words because describing it was faster than the model misunderstanding an image. That tradeoff flipped. I don't know when exactly. I just noticed one day that I'd stopped bothering to describe things.

Cost dropped somewhere around an order of magnitude in 18 months. Same output quality, ten times cheaper. That number sounds like a statistic but it changes the math on what's worth building. Things that were too expensive to run continuously are now fine. That's going to reshape a lot of things downstream and most of the reshaping hasn't happened yet.

## Three things I'd bet on

These are the curves I think are still compounding. I'll eat these words in two years if I'm wrong. I've eaten words before.

### Agents are the unit of work now, not prompts

Prompting as an interface is a transitional thing. It exists because we needed a way to talk to these systems before we figured out how to embed them in normal workflows. <mark>The prompt is the awkward first version of a much better interface.</mark>

What I expect over the next 18 months is that the valuable applications of AI stop looking like chat windows and start looking like persistent agents with goals, memory, and permission boundaries. Not chat that disappears. Chat that becomes the debugger while the agent is the product.

The hard part here isn't the loop itself. I've built simple agent flows and they're not technically complicated. The hard part is the trust model. What can it touch, what can it spend, what does it do when it's uncertain. That's mostly a design problem, not a capability problem. <mark>Design problems are solvable. That's actually an optimistic thing to say.</mark>

### Small fine-tuned models will eat the middle

For a long time the narrative was that only the frontier labs matter. The biggest model wins. I think the next 24 months tell a different story.

Most production work doesn't require general reasoning at the frontier level. Categorizing, summarizing, extracting, routing, flagging — the things that actually run at volume inside a real business — they don't need the most capable model in the world. <mark>They need something fast, cheap, and good enough to run continuously without a human in the loop.</mark> A fine-tuned 7B model on a specific domain beats a frontier model called on every request, in cost and often in quality, for that kind of task.

The frontier still matters. It sets the ceiling. But most actual work lives in the middle, and the middle is where small models are going to win.

### Evaluation is the real bottleneck

The most under-invested piece of the stack right now is knowing whether your model is actually doing its job.

You can tell when something is obviously broken. You cannot tell, reliably, when something is subtly wrong 2% of the time and getting worse. <mark>That 2% is where the real problems live.</mark> It's where a customer support agent quietly starts giving bad return policy answers. It's where a summarization tool starts dropping the part of the document that matters. You only find out from a complaint, not from your system.

I think in the next year this becomes a first-class discipline the same way observability did for infrastructure. Someone builds the equivalent of a Datadog for model behavior. Whoever does it well will matter more than a lot of the labs people currently spend time arguing about.

## Where I'm not sure

Things I've flip-flopped on in my private log and still don't have a strong view on.

Whether reasoning-focused models generalize beyond math and code or stay bounded to those domains. Whether on-device inference ever gets good enough to matter for consumer apps. Whether open-weight models stay competitive with the frontier or whether the gap keeps widening. Whether synthetic training data has a ceiling or whether it's just getting started.

<mark>I genuinely don't know. Anyone telling you they do is usually selling something.</mark>

## The human part

This is the section I find hardest to write without sounding either naive or doom-y. I'll try to say it plainly.

AI is not replacing people wholesale. It's also not leaving work untouched. What's actually happening, what I see in my own work every week, is that the definition of each role is being quietly rewritten. I spend less time on execution. I spend more time on judgment, direction, and taste. <mark>The things I get paid for have shifted, and they've shifted toward things that are harder to fake.</mark>

I think that's true across most knowledge work. Designers spend less time executing and more time deciding. Developers spend less time writing and more time reviewing. Writers spend less time on drafts and more time on structure.

The roles that are under real pressure are the ones where execution was the entire job. That's not everyone, but it's not no one either. I don't want to wave it away.

## The one thing I'm most sure of

Over the next five years, specific named problems that we currently consider too hard or too expensive to solve are going to start falling. Not all of them. Probably not even most of them. But enough that the cumulative effect will feel different than what we're used to.

It won't be because AI alone did it. <mark>It'll be because AI compressed the search space enough that small teams with the right tools can now do what used to require institutions.</mark> That compression is the real story. It's the thing I find genuinely exciting about this moment, separate from any particular model or benchmark.

I'm spending less time reading takes and more time building. If something changes my mind, I'll write it here.
