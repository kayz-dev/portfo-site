I was 15 when I built my first thing on the internet. Not a website, not an app. A GTA 5 roleplay server. This was 2020, deep in COVID, and like most Gen Z kids I had nowhere else to be. The world had funneled itself online and I followed it there.

It started as a way to make money. FiveM RP servers had real economies behind them. People paid for whitelist spots, custom scripts, server builds. I had no idea what I was doing, but I figured it out fast enough to get paid, and that was enough to keep going.

I want to be direct about something before I get into the lessons. I had no design background when I started. None. I did not come from a creative family, did not study art, did not know what kerning was. Everything I know now was learned out of necessity, obsession, and an enormous amount of repetition. I am saying this because I think people who write these kinds of posts often skip this part, and skipping it is dishonest.

## COVID and the shift we do not talk about enough

I think COVID was the actual entrance to the 21st century for most of us. Not symbolically. Literally. Every institution that could go digital did. Every business that had been dragging its feet on its online presence suddenly had no choice. Social behavior compressed into apps. Commerce shifted to feeds. The companies that peaked during that window were not lucky. They were positioned at the exact moment the remaining friction disappeared.

What I did not understand at the time was that I was doing the same thing at a much smaller scale. I was not building a business. I was learning the shape of a world that was reorganizing itself around digital experience, and I was doing it from the inside.

That context matters. We were robbed of a lot during that time: real social experience, physical space, the texture of being in your teens in public. But what replaced it was an accelerated understanding of how things work online. Some of us used that. Some of us did not. I am not sure there is a moral to that part of the story, but it is worth naming.

## What actually kept me going

There is a version of this post where I tell you I had a plan. I did not. What I had was a refusal to accept that I was not capable of understanding something. Every time I ran into a wall, I treated it as a gap in my knowledge rather than evidence I did not belong there. That sounds simple. It is not simple. It takes practice to not collapse the difference between "I do not know this yet" and "I cannot do this."

The three things that actually moved me forward:

### 1. One percent a day is not a motivational poster, it is math

There are 365 days in a year. If you improve by one percent every single day, you end the year 365 times more capable than you started. That is the floor. Most days you will not hit one percent. You will hit nothing, or go backwards. But some days you will hit thirty percent. You will discover something that rewires how you see a whole category of problem and you will not be able to stop working on it.

The math is not the point. The point is that the unit of measurement is days, not years. Years feel abstract. Days are manageable. On the days where everything feels slow, you just need to make the day not a zero. That is it. That is the whole thing.

I spent too much time early on measuring progress against people who had been doing this for a decade. That comparison is not just unhelpful, it is based on a category error. I was not behind them. I was me, 365 days into a process they were three thousand days into. The only comparison that matters is you from a year ago.

### 2. The little things are not small

I used to think in outcomes. I wanted the finished product, the polished brand, the thing that looked like the work I admired. What I did not understand was that outcomes are just the visible accumulation of decisions too small to notice individually.

The way I think about it now: every concept you learn has a structure, and that structure is built on other structures. If you try to learn typography without understanding proportion, or proportion without understanding rhythm, or rhythm without understanding how the eye actually moves, you end up with a fragile understanding that collapses under pressure. You can copy things that look good. You cannot make decisions when you are outside familiar territory.

The shift happened when I stopped trying to understand things at the surface and started trying to understand them from first principles. What is this actually doing. Why does it work. What would it take to break it. That process is slower. It is also the only one that compounds.

### 3. Know how you feel and why

This is the one people skip. I skipped it for years. It cost me time.

If you cannot understand your own state, you cannot perform. You cannot assess your work clearly. You cannot make decisions under pressure. You cannot tell the difference between "this is a bad day" and "this is a bad direction." Those two things require completely different responses, and conflating them is expensive.

I am not talking about therapy or journaling or any particular practice. I am talking about developing enough internal awareness that you know, in real time, what is affecting your output. That awareness is a skill. It is trainable. And in a field where the quality of your decisions is the product, it might be the highest-leverage skill there is.

We are in our early twenties now. We are experiencing things no generation experienced in quite this way: the compression of information, the restructuring of work, a world moving faster than any individual can fully track. The people who navigate this well are not going to be the most technically skilled. They are going to be the ones who understand themselves well enough to stay oriented when the environment is disorienting.

## Where I am now

I run a design and development studio. I build Shopify storefronts and digital products for brands that take the web seriously. I am 20, almost 21. I still do not think I have figured most of it out.

What I have figured out is the process. Not what to build, but how to stay in motion when you do not know what to build. How to use the days where nothing comes to sharpen something small. How to stay calibrated on what you actually know versus what you are pattern-matching from references.

That is what four years of showing up looks like from the inside.
